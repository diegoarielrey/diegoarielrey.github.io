From Constructors to Contact: A First-Principles Resolution of the Fermi Paradox

1) First Principles — Constructor Theory of Life

Life = information + physics.

Chiara Marletto’s Constructor Theory of Life shows that if the laws of physics allow information to be stored and copied, then replication, self-reproduction, and evolution follow naturally. No special “design” is required in the laws themselves.

The core idea is simple:

  • A replicator carries information and can be copied.
  • A vehicle is the physical machinery that performs the copying.
  • Together they form a self-reproducer.

The laws of physics need not contain anything biologically special. They only need to permit:

  1. Stable, copyable information (digital states).
  2. Energy flows that allow physical work to occur.

If those two conditions are met, then:

  • Replication becomes possible.
  • Replication with variation allows natural selection.
  • Selection allows increasing complexity.
  • Life emerges as a lawful outcome of physics.

The paper does not explain how life actually began. It proves something more fundamental: that life is compatible with ordinary, “no-design” physics.

Combine that with Schrödinger’s insight in What Is Life? — that life feeds on low-entropy energy gradients — and a plausible story appears:

  • Early chemistry produced crude polymers.
  • Some happened to assist their own copying.
  • Lipid compartments made copying more efficient.
  • Entropy gradients (like sunlight) powered the process.
  • Information accumulated.
  • Evolution began.

The key is information. Without information, chemistry merely reacts. With information, chemistry adapts.

That is the bridge from physics to life.

2) How Life Likely Began (Chemistry → Biology)

Section 1 shows that life is possible wherever physics allows copyable information and energy flow. The next question is how that transition likely unfolded in practice.

Stage A — Chemistry before information

Simple organic molecules (amino acids, amphiphiles, simple sugars, bases) formed through UV radiation, lightning, and hydrothermal chemistry. Environmental cycles (wet–dry, freeze–thaw) and mineral surfaces promoted polymerization, producing short, random chains.

At this stage, there was chemistry — but not yet heredity.

Stage B — The first replicators

Among these polymers, some happened to assist the formation of complementary chains. These molecules did not “intend” to copy themselves; rather, their structure increased the probability that similar structures would form again.

This is the first appearance of information: a physical pattern that influences its own re-creation.

Stage C — Compartments (vehicles)

Fatty acids spontaneously formed vesicles. When replicating polymers became encapsulated, copying became more efficient: substrates were concentrated, degradation slowed, and replication became coupled to vesicle growth and division.

This produced the first replicator–vehicle systems — the earliest self-reproducers.

Stage D — The RNA transition

Within these protocells, selection favored sequences that catalyzed reactions more efficiently and copied with greater fidelity. Chemistry shifted toward RNA-like molecules capable of both storing information and performing catalytic tasks (ribozymes).

Here, the abstract constructor-theoretic logic became chemically realized: the replicator (information) and the vehicle (machinery) increasingly internalized the copying process.

Stage E — DNA / Protein cells

Proteins eventually took over most catalysis, and DNA became a stable long-term information archive. Cells could now autonomously reproduce using environmental resources.

At this point, life — in the constructor-theoretic sense — was fully instantiated.

3) From Sensation to Brains (Why Minds Evolved)

Information-contingent behavior as the bridge:
Before brains can evolve, organisms must exhibit behavior that depends on environmental information in ways that affect replication.

The essential structure is:

  • Environmental variation → internal state change
  • Internal state change → biased behavior
  • Biased behavior → change in replication probability

This feedback loop is the critical evolutionary threshold. Without it:

  • Behavior is fixed.
  • Environmental differences do not alter reproductive success through action.
  • Selection cannot favor improved sensing or control.
  • There is nothing for a nervous system to elaborate.

Primitive forms of this loop can arise in simple systems (e.g. chemotaxis), but the specific mechanism is not essential. What matters is that environmental information influences action in ways that affect fitness.

Prediction pays:
Once environmental information shapes behavior, selection favors systems that:

  • sense more accurately,
  • integrate signals across time,
  • anticipate future states,
  • select among possible actions.

Nervous systems centralize and scale this architecture. Brains are higher-order information-processing constructors that refine this feedback loop — improving control over energy acquisition, survival, and ultimately accelerating knowledge creation.

4) Viable non-Earth conditions for life (gradient × substrate)

Life requires a low-entropy energy flux and an information medium; the substrate can vary.

  • Radiative (photon) worlds: hot-to-cold photon gradients; organics or photopolymers on temperate surfaces.
  • Chemo-redox worlds: vent or subsurface oceans; metal-sulfide catalysis; slower but steady.
  • Thermal-cycle worlds: strong geothermal/tidal cycling; polymerization on mineral matrices.
  • Electrical/plasma life: field-driven organization (fast info flow; stability is the challenge).
  • Solid-state/mineral life: information in lattice defects/charge patterns; adaptation likely slower.
  • Cryogenic solvents (ammonia/methane): very slow kinetics; long stability, slow evolution.

Why Earth-like is fastest to AI: moderate temperatures (~250–350 K), liquid water, transparent atmosphere + dry land (fire, metallurgy), oxygenation (high-power metabolism), diverse niches, and long climate stability together maximize evolutionary tempo toward technology.

5) Why AI/autonomy is (practically) necessary for interstellar travel

  • Timescales/latency: decades–millennia missions require on-board diagnosis, repair, replanning.
  • Closed-loop maintenance: habitats and vehicles need continual, adaptive control.
  • Navigation in unknowns: hazard avoidance, deceleration, in-situ resource use.

You can launch without AI, but robust interstellar capability becomes realistic only with open-ended autonomy (i.e., AI-like constructors).

6) The cosmic clock: when could life/AI appear?

  • Heavy elements → rocky planets: not before ~2 billion years after the Big Bang.
  • Biological ascent: Earth took ~4 billion years from origin of life to technology; faster paths might still need ≥1–2 billion years.

Implication: AI-capable civilizations could first appear a few billion years after the first metal-rich planets formed; i.e., earliest feasible AI civilizations could predate Earth by hundreds of millions to a couple of billion years, but we are still early in the long habitable era.

7) Why the nearest galaxy is the earliest plausible source of contact (not the Milky Way)

If continuous interstellar expansion follows capability, then inside one galaxy a probe wave crosses in ~1–10 million years (0.1–0.01 c).

We see no Galactic-scale signatures → simplest reading: no one in the Milky Way started such a wave ≥10 Myr before us.

Extra-galactic arrivals: the nearest large galaxy (Andromeda, ~2.5 million ly) needs ~25–250 million years travel time at 0.1–0.01 c. A modest head-start over Earth (by ≥25–250 Myr) makes “arrival by now” physically possible, though not compelled (targeting/motivation/cost).

8) Final timeline: earliest→latest contact window & why

Rationale: earliest metal-rich planets → earliest AI → flight time to Earth.

Earliest feasible arrivals: if a neighbor galaxy reached AI early and launched fast craft (0.1 c), arrivals could have been ~25 million years after launch (e.g., Andromeda).

Later/plausible arrivals: at 0.01 c, ~250 million years after launch (Andromeda/M33).

Slow craft: 0.001 c implies ~2.5 billion years from Andromeda.

Expressed simply (relative to “now”):

Could have arrived as early as: ~25–250 million years ago (if launched then, at 0.1–0.01 c, from Andromeda/M33).
Could still arrive as late as: up to ~2 500 million (2.5 billion) years from now (if 0.001 c from Andromeda; farther origins scale to ~10⁸–10⁹ years at 0.01 c).

Why this range: it’s set by (i) when a nearby large galaxy could plausibly have produced an AI civilization (head-start over Earth by 10⁸–10⁹ yr) plus (ii) how fast they travel (0.1–0.001 c) plus (iii) distance (M31 ≈ 2.5 Mly). Inside our own galaxy the crossing time is only 1–10 Myr, so our present silence implies no earlier Milky-Way launcher under a continuous-expansion assumption.
