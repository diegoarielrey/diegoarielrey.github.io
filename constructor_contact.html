1) First principles (Constructor Theory of Life)

Life as a task: under “no-design” laws, life is a physical system that can cause

\[ (S, N) \rightarrow (S, S, W) \]

— i.e., self-reproduction from generic resources. A replicator (information) plus a vehicle (machinery) is sufficient; together they are the self-reproducer.

Replication task:

\[ \bigcup_{X \in \Sigma} \{ (X, N) \rightarrow (X, X, W) \} \]

No-design laws: generic resources perform only elementary tasks; no built-in biological designs.

Key requirement: physics must allow digital information media (copyable, permutable states). If so, replication, self-reproduction and natural selection are possible in principle.

Takeaway: once physics allows copyable information, the replicator-vehicle architecture can exist; evolution then operates.

2) A granular “how life began” (chemistry → biology)

Stage A — Abiotic feedstocks & polymerization
Simple organics (e.g., amino acids, amphiphiles, simple sugars, bases) form via UV/lighting/vent chemistry. Cycling environments (wet–dry, freeze–thaw) and mineral surfaces catalyze polymerization into short, random pre-RNA polymers (peptides; PNA/TNA/GNA).

Stage B — First replicators (pre-RNA)
Some polymers template complementary chains (environment-assisted copying). Early “replicators” = structures whose patterns promote making more of the same pattern (still without an internal vehicle).

Stage C — First vehicles (compartments)
Fatty-acid vesicles form spontaneously and encapsulate replicators. Encapsulation concentrates substrates, stabilizes chemistry, and couples replicator success to vesicle growth/division — the first replicator–vehicle systems

\[ S = (R, V) \]

Stage D — Toward RNA
Within protocells, selection favors sequences with better catalysis and fidelity. Chemistry shifts toward RNA-like monomers; ribozymes appear (information + catalysis). This realizes the constructor-theoretic logic (copy \(R\), build \(V\)) to increasing accuracy. (Marletto shows copy & build are the two essential phases.)

Stage E — DNA/protein cells
Proteins take over catalysis; DNA becomes the long-term archive. Cells now perform

\[ (S, N) \rightarrow (S, S, W) \]

autonomously — fully alive in constructor-theoretic terms.

3) From sensation to brains (why minds evolved)

Chemotaxis as the bridge: coupling receptors to motion biases drift up nutrient gradients; any feedback that improves energy capture is selected.

Prediction pays: nervous systems centralize sensing→prediction→action, improving energetic efficiency. Brains are information-processing constructors that maximize survival by better exploiting gradients (and ultimately accelerate knowledge creation).

4) Viable non-Earth conditions for life (gradient × substrate)

Life requires a low-entropy energy flux and an information medium; the substrate can vary.

  • Radiative (photon) worlds: hot-to-cold photon gradients; organics or photopolymers on temperate surfaces.
  • Chemo-redox worlds: vent or subsurface oceans; metal-sulfide catalysis; slower but steady.
  • Thermal-cycle worlds: strong geothermal/tidal cycling; polymerization on mineral matrices.
  • Electrical/plasma life: field-driven organization (fast info flow; stability is the challenge).
  • Solid-state/mineral life: information in lattice defects/charge patterns; adaptation likely slower.
  • Cryogenic solvents (ammonia/methane): very slow kinetics; long stability, slow evolution.

Why Earth-like is fastest to AI: moderate temperatures (~250–350 K), liquid water, transparent atmosphere + dry land (fire, metallurgy), oxygenation (high-power metabolism), diverse niches, and long climate stability together maximize evolutionary tempo toward technology.

5) Why AI/autonomy is (practically) necessary for interstellar travel

  • Timescales/latency: decades–millennia missions require on-board diagnosis, repair, replanning.
  • Closed-loop maintenance: habitats and vehicles need continual, adaptive control.
  • Navigation in unknowns: hazard avoidance, deceleration, in-situ resource use.

You can launch without AI, but robust interstellar capability becomes realistic only with open-ended autonomy (i.e., AI-like constructors).

6) The cosmic clock: when could life/AI appear?

  • Heavy elements → rocky planets: not before ~2 billion years after the Big Bang.
  • Biological ascent: Earth took ~4 billion years from origin of life to technology; faster paths might still need ≥1–2 billion years.

Implication: AI-capable civilizations could first appear a few billion years after the first metal-rich planets formed; i.e., earliest feasible AI civilizations could predate Earth by hundreds of millions to a couple of billion years, but we are still early in the long habitable era.

7) Why the nearest galaxy is the earliest plausible source of contact (not the Milky Way)

If continuous interstellar expansion follows capability, then inside one galaxy a probe wave crosses in ~1–10 million years (0.1–0.01 c).

We see no Galactic-scale signatures → simplest reading: no one in the Milky Way started such a wave ≥10 Myr before us.

Extra-galactic arrivals: the nearest large galaxy (Andromeda, ~2.5 million ly) needs ~25–250 million years travel time at 0.1–0.01 c. A modest head-start over Earth (by ≥25–250 Myr) makes “arrival by now” physically possible, though not compelled (targeting/motivation/cost).

8) Final timeline: earliest→latest contact window & why

Rationale: earliest metal-rich planets → earliest AI → flight time to Earth.

Earliest feasible arrivals: if a neighbor galaxy reached AI early and launched fast craft (0.1 c), arrivals could have been ~25 million years after launch (e.g., Andromeda).

Later/plausible arrivals: at 0.01 c, ~250 million years after launch (Andromeda/M33).

Slow craft: 0.001 c implies ~2.5 billion years from Andromeda.

Expressed simply (relative to “now”):

Could have arrived as early as: ~25–250 million years ago (if launched then, at 0.1–0.01 c, from Andromeda/M33).
Could still arrive as late as: up to ~2 500 million (2.5 billion) years from now (if 0.001 c from Andromeda; farther origins scale to ~10⁸–10⁹ years at 0.01 c).

Why this range: it’s set by (i) when a nearby large galaxy could plausibly have produced an AI civilization (head-start over Earth by 10⁸–10⁹ yr) plus (ii) how fast they travel (0.1–0.001 c) plus (iii) distance (M31 ≈ 2.5 Mly). Inside our own galaxy the crossing time is only 1–10 Myr, so our present silence implies no earlier Milky-Way launcher under a continuous-expansion assumption.
